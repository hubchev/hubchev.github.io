Appendix A — Glossary

Budget constraint line: Shows the possible combinations of two goods that are affordable given a consumer’s limited income.

Complements: Goods that go together; a decrease in the price of one results in an increase in demand for the other, and vice versa.

Competitive market: A market in which there are many buyers and many sellers, so each has a negligible impact on the market price.

Consumer equilibrium: Occurs when the ratio of the prices of goods is equal to the ratio of the marginal utilities, representing the point at which the consumer achieves maximum satisfaction.

Deadweight loss: The loss in social surplus that occurs when a market produces an inefficient quantity.

Demand curve: A graph that illustrates how much of a given product a household would be willing to buy at different prices.

Demand schedule: A table showing the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded.

Diminishing marginal utility: The common pattern whereby each marginal unit of a good consumed provides less additional utility than the previous unit.

Elasticity: A general concept used to quantify the response in one variable when another variable changes.

Equilibrium: The condition that exists when quantity supplied and quantity demanded are equal; at this point, there is no tendency for the price to change.

Excess demand or shortage: The condition that exists when quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied at the current price.

Excess supply or surplus: The condition that occurs when quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded at the current price.

Giffen good: A good for which, all else equal, an increase in price leads to an increase in demand, violating the basic law of demand.

Inferior good: A good for which, all else equal, an increase in income leads to a decrease in demand.

Labor supply: Usually refers to the amount of time workers are willing to work at a given wage.

Labor demand: Generally refers to the amount of time employers are willing to hire labor at a given wage.

Law of demand: The claim that, all else equal, the quantity demanded of a good falls when the price of the good rises.

Law of supply: The claim that, all else equal, the quantity supplied of a good rises when the price of the good rises.

Marginal utility: The additional utility provided by one additional unit of consumption.

Marginal utility per dollar: The additional satisfaction gained from purchasing a good, given the price of that product (MU/Price).

Market: A group of buyers and sellers of a particular good or service.

Normal good: A good for which, all else equal, an increase in income or a decrease in price leads to an increase in demand.

Perfectly competitive market: Defined by two main characteristics: (1) the goods offered for sale are identical, and (2) the number of buyers and sellers is large enough that no single buyer or seller can influence the market price.

Perfect substitutes: Identical goods that can replace each other.

Price ceiling: A maximum price that sellers may charge for a good, usually set by the government; a legal maximum price.

Price control: Government regulations to manage prices instead of allowing market forces to determine them.

Price floor: A legal minimum price.

Producer surplus: The extra benefit producers receive from selling a good or service, measured by the price received minus the price they would have been willing to accept.

Quantity demanded: The amount of a good that buyers are willing and able to purchase.

Quantity supplied: The amount of a good that sellers are willing and able to sell.

Substitutes: Goods that can replace one another; when the price of one increases, demand for the other goes up.

Supply: The willingness and ability of producers to create goods and services for market sale.

Supply curve: A graph illustrating how much of a given product a firm will supply at different prices.

Total utility: The satisfaction derived from consumer choices.

Demand: Refers to the willingness and ability of consumers to purchase a given quantity of a good or service at a specific point in time or over a period.

Exports: Goods and services sold to other countries.

Imports: Goods and services bought from other countries.

Trade balance: The difference between the value of goods and services a country sells abroad and those it buys from abroad, also known as net exports.

Trade surplus: When a country sells more than it buys, resulting in a positive trade balance.

Trade deficit: When a country buys more than it sells, leading to a negative trade balance.

Balanced trade: When the value of exports equals the value of imports.

Net capital outflow: The difference between the purchase of foreign assets by domestic residents and the purchase of domestic assets by foreigners. This equals net exports, indicating that a country’s savings can fund investments domestically or abroad. We will elaborate on that later on in greater detail.
